# Ω Distribution Maps

## Purpose

A single Ω value is insufficient to characterize structural behavior.

This document defines the distribution-based analysis layer for OMNIA-CONSTANT.

The goal is to study how Ω distributes across different classes of systems.

---

## Core idea

Instead of asking:

```text
Is Ω≈0.6 present?

OMNIA-CONSTANT also asks:

How does Ω distribute across domains, perturbations, and structural conditions?

The distribution itself may contain stronger structural information than isolated values.


---

Primary hypothesis

Different classes of systems may produce distinct Ω distribution geometries.

Examples:

pure randomness

weak persistence

mixed structure/noise

stable recursive systems

adversarial pseudo-structures

real-world noisy systems



---

Distribution classes

D0 · Random Distribution

Expected properties:

unstable Ω

broad spread

weak clustering

no persistent concentration near Ω≈0.6


Possible shape:

flat / noisy / unstable


---

D1 · Weak Persistence Distribution

Expected properties:

intermittent clustering

partial concentration near transition regions

unstable persistence


Possible shape:

broad cluster near persistence boundary


---

D2 · Transition Distribution

Expected properties:

concentration near instability/persistence transition

sensitivity to perturbation

mixed structural behavior


Possible shape:

boundary accumulation around Ω≈0.6


---

D3 · Strong Persistence Distribution

Expected properties:

stable Ω values

reduced variance

persistence above boundary region


Possible shape:

high-Ω concentration


---

D4 · Adversarial False Persistence

Expected properties:

artificial clustering

unstable reproduction

metric dependence

collapse under deeper testing


Possible shape:

fake concentration with weak robustness


---

Important question

Does Ω≈0.6 emerge as a natural transition region
between instability and persistence?

This is stronger than:

Ω=0.6 appears sometimes


---

Distribution geometry

Each distribution should be analyzed for:

mean

variance

skewness

kurtosis

clustering

multimodality

persistence bands

transition density

collapse regions

adversarial overlap



---

Structural interpretation

Possible interpretations:

low Ω region
→ instability / randomness

boundary Ω region
→ marginal persistence

high Ω region
→ stronger structural persistence

These interpretations remain provisional.


---

Cross-domain comparison

Distributions should be compared across:

symbolic systems

graphs

dynamical systems

financial systems

crypto systems

biological systems

LLM perturbation systems

synthetic collapse systems

random baselines



---

Failure condition

Reject distribution significance if:

random systems produce indistinguishable distributions

adversarial systems reproduce the same geometry

distributions collapse under metric substitution

distributions disappear under scale changes

distributions are not reproducible



---

Important boundary

A visible cluster is not automatically meaningful.

Distribution geometry may emerge from:

preprocessing artifacts

metric design

finite-size effects

normalization bias

representation compression


All observed structures remain falsifiable.


---

Core principle

measurement != interpretation != decision
